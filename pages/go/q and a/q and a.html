Excerpt from https://golang.org/doc/faq

  • Q: Does Go have a runtime?
    A: Yes, but Go's runtime is analogous to libc in C, not a virtual machine in Java

  • Q: Why does Go not have generic types?
    A: They are convenient but they come at a cost in complexity

  • Q: Why does Go not have exceptions?
     A: We believe that coupling exceptions to a control structure, as in the try-catch-finally idiom, results in convoluted code

  • Q: Why does Go not support overloading of methods and operators?
    A: It was occasionally useful but that it could also be confusing and fragile in practice

  • Q: Why does Go not provide implicit numeric conversions?
    A: It complicates the compiler and result is not portable

  • Q: Why is my trivial program such a large binary?
     A: All Go binaries include the Go runtime, along with the run-time type information necessary to support dynamic type checks, reflection, and even panic-time stack traces

  • Q: Why is there no pointer arithmetic?
     A: Safety

  • Q: Can I stop these complaints about my unused variable/import?
     A: The presence of an unused variable may indicate a bug and Go compiler did not support warning

  • Q: Why Go compiler does not report warnings, only errors?
     A:  First, it's worth complaining about.  Second, warning about weak case can be a noise that masking real errors that should be fixed

  • Q: Why do garbage collection? Won't it be too expensive?
     A: Managing the lifetimes of allocated objects is one of the biggest bookkeeping and is often the cause of bugs.  Even in languages like C++ or Rust that provide mechanisms to assist, it often adding programming overhead of its own. Advances in garbage collection technology gave us confidence that it could be implemented cheaply enough
